Understanding the process
Who can foster?
Almost anyone over 21 can apply to foster in the UK. You don't need to own your home, be married, or have children of your own. Single people, people who rent, people with mental health histories -- all can and do foster successfully. What matters is your capacity to provide a stable, caring environment. Your own experience of adversity can be a strength, not a disqualification.
The assessment process
The process typically takes 4-6 months. A social worker visits your home multiple times, explores your background, childhood, relationships, and motivation. They're not looking for perfection -- they're looking for self-awareness, resilience, and capacity for empathy. Being in therapy and having done the healing work you've done is seen as a positive, not a concern. They want people who understand themselves.
Skills to Foster training
Before approval, you'll complete a training course (usually called "Skills to Foster" or similar). It covers child development, attachment theory, managing challenging behaviour, working with birth families, and self-care. Many of the concepts -- attachment, nervous system regulation, trauma responses -- are things you already understand from your own journey. You'll likely find you know more than you expect.
Types of foster care
Emergency: Short-notice placements (hours/days) for children in immediate need.

Short-term: Weeks to months while a longer-term plan is made.

Long-term: Providing a stable home for years, sometimes until adulthood.

Respite: Giving other foster carers a break -- short stays, usually planned.

Specialist: For children with specific needs, disabilities, or complex trauma histories.

You get to choose the type that fits your life and capacity. There's no obligation to do everything.
Financial support
Foster carers receive a fostering allowance to cover the cost of caring for a child, plus a fee/reward element for your time and skills. Rates vary by local authority and the child's needs but typically range from around 350-700+ per week. The first 10,000 per year of qualifying care relief is tax-free. Fostering can be a viable way to support yourself while doing meaningful work.
Support available to you
As a foster carer, you won't be alone. You'll have a supervising social worker, access to training, peer support groups, and often a 24/7 helpline. Many agencies provide therapeutic support for both you and the child. The support network is designed to ensure you can sustain this work without burning out. You're not expected to do it alone -- and the fact that you already know how to ask for help (hello, Anahi) is a strength.
